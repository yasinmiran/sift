**What matters today:** Apple sued OpenAI, alleging trade secret theft "at every level" of the company, from rank-and-file hires up to chief hardware officer Tang Tan, a 24-year Apple veteran accused of directing job candidates to bring actual Apple parts to interviews and coaching departing staff on evading security procedures; Apple's filing also names ex-Apple engineer Chang Liu and Jony Ive's startup io Products and claims ==400+ former Apple employees== now work at OpenAI, which says it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets." OpenAI had a rougher day elsewhere too: its head of safety, Johannes Heidecke, is leaving as the safety team folds into research under newly promoted VP Mia Glaese (Greg Brockman absorbs Fidji Simo's product oversight rather than see her replaced), and a claimed proof of the long-unsolved Cycle Double Cover Conjecture from GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra drew immediate caution, OpenAI itself says it needs weeks of independent stress-testing before anyone should trust it. Elsewhere, SK Hynix's Wall Street debut collided with its own CEO's supply-shortage warning, a New York Times-backed investigation found Boko Haram members querying AI chatbots for weapons help, and three unrelated physics and space stories landed the same day: a Chinese rocket booster's first sea-based net recovery, Google's Willow chip learning to recalibrate itself mid-computation, and the most precise test yet of Einstein's frame-dragging prediction.

## AI / LLMs

- [OpenAI made its AI biosafety bug bounty a permanent program and doubled the top reward to $50,000](https://alphasignal.ai/news/openai-makes-its-gpt-5-6-bio-bounty-permanent-and-doubles-prize-to-50-000), covering GPT-5.6 going forward; separately, [Artificial Analysis's independent AA-Briefcase benchmark gave GPT-5.6 Sol the best presentation score of any model tested](https://alphasignal.ai/news/openai-s-gpt-5-6-sol-dominates-real-business-work-with-best-ever-presentation), citing roughly 1.6x better token efficiency and about half the cost of rivals on the same tasks.
- [OpenAI published a PDF claiming GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra produced a proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture](https://cdn.openai.com/pdf/04d1d1e4-bc75-476a-97cf-49055cd98d31/cdc_proof.pdf) using 64 subagents in under an hour, a graph theory problem open since the 1970s; the result is ((not yet peer-reviewed)), OpenAI itself frames it as needing weeks of mathematician scrutiny, and the conjecture has a history of claimed-then-retracted proofs on arXiv since 2015.
- OpenAI's safety team is being folded into research: [head of safety Johannes Heidecke is leaving](https://www.techmeme.com/260710/p38#a260710p38) as Mia Glaese becomes VP of research and safety and Saachi Jain takes over as interim head of safety systems, the latest OpenAI leadership move after product chief Fidji Simo's exit this week; [Greg Brockman, already company president, will keep overseeing products directly](https://www.techmeme.com/260710/p24#a260710p24) rather than see Simo replaced.
- [China Mobile released JT-4.1 Flash, a 236B-parameter model priced at zero dollars per million tokens via API](https://alphasignal.ai/news/china-mobile-s-jt-4-1-flash-challenges-deepseek-with-a-free-236b-model), positioned against DeepSeek on cost even though, unlike DeepSeek's MIT-licensed weights, JT-4.1 Flash's weights stay proprietary.
- [Perplexity's Agent API added Grok 4.5 as a selectable model](https://alphasignal.ai/news/perplexity-swaps-claude-for-grok-4-5-at-half-the-cost), priced at roughly half GPT-5.5's per-task cost on coding benchmarks.
- [Meta's FlashNormAttention fuses RMSNorm into the attention and feed-forward kernels of a transformer block](https://alphasignal.ai/news/meta-s-flashnormattention-hides-90-of-transformer-normalization-costs) to cut memory-bandwidth and kernel-launch overhead from normalization; separately, [Upstage's Solar 31B hit 2,000 tokens per second on Cerebras' wafer-scale inference hardware](https://alphasignal.ai/news/upstage-s-solar-31b-hits-2-000-tokens-per-second-on-cerebras-hardware), part of a push to bring fast local inference to South Korea.

## Devtools & Infra

- [Cursor 3.11 added "side chats"](https://alphasignal.ai/news/cursor-3-11-lets-developers-run-parallel-ai-chats-without-stopping-their-agent), durable parallel agent conversations that inherit context from the main chat and can be pulled back in with an @-mention, without pausing the primary run.
- [A June firmware update (2071353020) left some Philips Hue Bridge Pro smart-home hubs bricked with a solid red LED](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2026/07/firmware-update-bricks-hue-bridge-pro-devices-philips-gives-free-replacements/), Philips confirms; the company is sending free replacements to affected customers. Separately, [Valve's new "Steam Machine Verified" ratings are silent on a messy middle tier of games](https://arstechnica.com/gaming/2026/07/valves-steam-machine-verified-ratings-offer-more-questions-than-answers/): titles SteamOS can run fine but the older Steam Deck can't hit its 1200x800/30fps bar get marked merely "unsupported" on Deck, with no Steam Machine verdict of their own yet.

## Security & Privacy

- [A CISA contractor exposed roughly 844MB of internal CISA/DHS data on a personal GitHub repo for about six months](https://www.techmeme.com/260710/p33#a260710p33), including AWS GovCloud admin credentials, plaintext passwords, SAML signing certificates, and SSH keys, after disabling GitHub's default secret-scanning protection; CISA says it learned of the exposure only when a journalist asked for comment. Separately, [a ransomware negotiator hired by DigitalMint to protect clients from BlackCat attackers was secretly feeding those attackers confidential negotiation intel to inflate the ransoms](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2026/07/ransomware-negotiator-helped-attackers-extort-his-own-clients-gets-6-year-sentence/): five victims paid over $75 million as a result, and Angelo Martino was sentenced to 70 months, following two co-defendants sentenced earlier this year.
- [A New York Times investigation, built on interviews with 27 former Boko Haram members, found the group has moved past using AI chatbots for propaganda into querying them for weapons design help, weapons upgrades, and battlefield problem-solving](https://www.techmeme.com/260711/p5#a260711p5), with members reportedly obtaining otherwise-restricted answers by disguising the intent behind their questions, the same use-at-scale risk that pushed OpenAI to permanently fund its biosafety bounty this year.
- [Google urged the European Commission not to target DNS resolvers, VPNs, or shared IPs in anti-piracy rules](https://www.techmeme.com/260711/p7#a260711p7), calling site-blocking at that level "ineffective" (easily circumvented by switching DNS resolvers) and "disproportionate," pointing to Italy's Piracy Shield blocking a Google Drive subdomain and a 2019 Portuguese order that disrupted legitimate Google Cloud traffic.

## Startups & Industry

- [Apple sued OpenAI over alleged systemic trade secret theft](https://www.theverge.com/tech/964350/apple-openai-lawsuit-trade-secrets), naming chief hardware officer Tang Tan and engineer Chang Liu, who Apple says kept a company laptop after leaving and used a cloud storage bug to download dozens of confidential hardware files; [OpenAI responded that it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets."](https://9to5mac.com/2026/07/10/apple-sues-openai-trade-secret-theft/)
- [Meta discontinued the Instagram Muse feature that let any user generate AI images of public accounts without consent](https://www.theverge.com/tech/964416/meta-instagram-ai-muse-image-deepfakes), days after its Tuesday launch drew backlash from talent agencies and union officials; Meta says the feature "missed the mark."
- [SK Hynix's CEO warned 2027 will be the memory industry's worst-ever supply year](https://www.techmeme.com/260710/p36#a260710p36), with customer demand outstripping the company's production capacity beyond 2030, two days after its record foreign IPO debut on Nasdaq; Bloomberg frames the listing itself as a bet that the AI boom has broken the industry's decades-long boom-and-bust cycle for good.
- [New York City announced a "Click to Cancel" rule requiring subscription cancellation to be as easy as signup](https://www.nyc.gov/mayors-office/news/2026/07/mayor-mamdani-announces-landmark--click-to-cancel--consumer-prot), taking effect October 1 with penalties starting at $525 per violation and an estimated $162.5 million in annual consumer savings.
- [Crunchbase's weekly funding roundup found AI claiming five of the ten biggest US rounds](https://news.crunchbase.com/ai/biggest-funding-rounds-billion-dollar-cyber-ai-keyfactor-sambanova/), led by a pair of billion-dollar deals: digital-identity security firm Keyfactor's $1B private equity round and AI-chip and infrastructure maker SambaNova's $1B Series F at an $11B valuation.
- [The Trump administration pushed a heavy-handed campaign to prop up Intel](https://www.techmeme.com/260711/p2#a260711p2), including pressuring Apple and Nvidia toward Intel's fabs after converting a $9B federal grant into roughly a 10% equity stake in the chipmaker, the Wall Street Journal reports.

## Research

- [China recovered a Long March-10B first-stage booster at sea using a net, not a powered vertical landing](https://arstechnica.com/space/2026/07/china-recovered-its-first-reusable-rocket-and-showed-a-new-way-to-do-it/), the first net-based rocket recovery; the reusable configuration carries up to 16,000kg to low Earth orbit versus Falcon 9's 22,800kg.
- [Google trained a reinforcement-learning agent to continuously recalibrate its Willow quantum processor mid-computation](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/07/quantum-error-correction-can-constantly-recalibrate-a-processor/), repurposing error-detection events as a live training signal instead of pausing for periodic recalibration.
- [The LARES satellite, a 362kg tungsten sphere covered in laser reflectors, delivered the most precise measurement yet of frame-dragging](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/07/an-orbiting-disco-ball-gave-einsteins-theory-its-most-precise-test-yet/), confirming Einstein's prediction that Earth's rotation drags spacetime with it.

## Threads

- AI's darker uses kept surfacing alongside its productivity wins: Boko Haram querying chatbots for weapons help, a contractor's careless GitHub habits exposing CISA's own infrastructure, and a ransomware negotiator's betrayal of his own clients, three failures of trust rather than of code.
- The memory and compute buildout sent mixed signals at once: SK Hynix's framing of its listing as breaking the industry's boom-bust cycle collided with its own CEO's warning of the worst supply shortage yet, while Meta's FlashNormAttention and Upstage's Cerebras milestone chased cheaper inference from the other direction.
- Consumer hardware had a rough day on reliability: Philips' bricked Hue hubs and Valve's incomplete Steam Machine ratings both left buyers holding devices their makers hadn't fully tested.
- Regulators pulled on the internet's plumbing from both ends: Google lobbying the EU to leave DNS resolvers and VPNs out of anti-piracy rules, while NYC moved the other way, mandating one-click subscription cancellation.
